Since Aristotle, natural philosophy has been divided into inquiry into principles and causes, motion and infinity, space and time. 📖 1.1 Problematic of existence

The question "What is existence?" has always been the core of philosophical thought since before Socrates. Parmenides presented a vision of existence as one unchanging, while Heraclitus saw change as the essence. This duality has haunted Western thought: Is existence a constant substance or a flowing process? Is it one or many? Can the mind grasp it or are veils permanent?

With modern physics, these questions have been renewed. Relativity showed that space and time are not absolute, but connected in a single structure: spacetime. Quantum mechanics revealed a world of superposition and probability, where no actual existence exists before measurement. Particle physics asks again: What is mass? What is charge? What makes an electron an electron?

System 𝕊 offers a framework that answers these questions based on a fundamental triad: the Absolute (❶), Possibility (0ₚ), and Relation (∞). This triad is not merely a philosophical construct, but a mathematical structure that generates numbers, constants, and predictions.

2

📖 1.2 Crisis of mathematical foundations

At the beginning of the 20th century, David Hilbert sought to completely formalize mathematics, i.e., to build a consistent and complete axiomatic system that would guarantee no contradictions and allow proving or disproving any mathematical statement. But this project received fatal blows:

  1. Russell's paradox (1901): The set of all sets that do not contain themselves – does it contain itself? The answer leads to a contradiction. This revealed that naive intuition can generate contradictions if not regulated.
  2. Gödel's theorems (1931): Any formal system that includes arithmetic (like ZFC) is either incomplete or inconsistent. There are true statements that cannot be proved within the system, and the consistency cannot be proved from within. This put a final end to the Hilbert program.
  3. Cohen's independence (1963): The continuum hypothesis is independent of ZFC, i.e., it can be accepted or rejected without contradiction.

These results showed that formal foundations alone are insufficient. There is a need for ontological foundations that connect mathematics to reality, and offer a criterion for truth beyond formal consistency. This is what System 𝕊 provides.

📖 1.3 The necessity of returning to metaphysics

If mathematics is incapable of founding itself, and if physics constantly reformulates its ontological questions, then we have no choice but to return to metaphysics – not in its traditional sense separated from science, but as a reflection on the structure of existence guided by both mathematics and physics.

System 𝕊 offers a mathematical metaphysics based on:

  • Principle of the One: Every existent bears the imprint of the Absolute ❶, and its degree of proximity determines its stability.
  • Principle of Possibility: Existence is not binary (existent/non‑existent), but there is a middle region: pure possibility 0ₚ, in which all possibilities are manifested before being actualized.
  • Principle of Relation: Existence is not isolated substances, but a network of relations ∞ that determines the identity of everything.

These three principles allow us to reread the history of philosophy (from Pythagoras to Heidegger) and the history of physics (from Newton to string theory) in a unified light.
